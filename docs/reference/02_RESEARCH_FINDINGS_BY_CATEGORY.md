# Research Findings by Category

## Main Takeaway

Most climbing-specific products and practical literature emphasize:

- maximal force
- rate of force development
- repeater endurance
- total force-time characteristics

They usually do not measure force per finger.

## Climbing-Specific Findings

Climbing-specific sources are useful for:

- benchmark design
- force-time framing
- max and repeater structures
- training usability and interpretation

Their main limitation for GripSense is that they often treat the hand as one output rather than a set of finger-specific contributors.

## Adjacent Per-Finger Findings

More directly relevant per-finger concepts appear across:

- rehab
- ergonomics
- hand biomechanics
- robotics
- instrumented grip research

Useful themes include:

- individual finger strength
- multi-finger grip force
- grip force distribution
- finger load sharing
- asymmetry
- fatigue and drift
- pressure and compensation patterns

## Why This Matters For GripSense

This suggests two opportunities at once:

- a market gap
- a research-translation gap

The underlying measurement idea is credible, but the climbing market has not standardized a practical per-finger field tool.

## Measurement And Validation Implications

A serious product in this space still needs explicit documentation for:

- sampling rate
- calibration
- repeatability
- drift
- hysteresis
- signal noise
- cross-talk between channels

## Practical Use For The Repo

Use climbing-specific sources mainly for:

- protocol families
- performance framing
- familiar training/test structures

Use adjacent per-finger sources mainly for:

- measurement strategy
- sensor and validation logic
- rehab relevance
- the product's unique value story
